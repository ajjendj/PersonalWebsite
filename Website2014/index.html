ABOUT


I am currently a PhD student working under the supervision of Dr. Margrit Betke and Dr. Stan Sclaroff with the Image and Video Computing research group at Boston University's Department of Computer Science. I attained a Master of Science degree in Computer Science from Boston University in 2014, and a Bachelor of Arts degree from Connecticut College in 2012 where I majored in Computer Science and Architectural Studies and completed a certificate program in Arts and Technology.

My broad research interests lie in computer vision, machine learning, and human computer interaction. Particularly, I am interested in the analysis of spatio-temporal signals generated by human motion. In my spare time, I do freelance work in art and photography. Some of my work can be seen at ajjenjoshi.com.

RESEARCH

MULTIMODAL ASSESSMENT OF CONVERSATIONAL ENGAGEMENT IN PERSONS WITH PARKINSON'S DISEASE (current)


I am devising a machine learning framework capable of predicting behavioral engagement scores of people suffering from motor impairments by studying descriptors extracted from multiple modalities (video, audio, text) of persons engaged in conversation. A computational tool capable of doing so would help therapists diagnose and evaluate patients, as well as identify specific features responsible for the assessment score in order to provide individualized therapy.

PROGRESS REPORT
A RANDOM FOREST APPROACH TO SEGMENTING AND CLASSIFYING GESTURES (2014)


For my MS thesis, I investigated a gesture segmentation and recognition scheme that employed a random forest classification model. Our approach performs the tasks of temporal segmentation and classification simultaneously. Given an input video stream, our trained model is applied to candidate gestures using sliding windows at multiple temporal scales. The class label with the highest classier confidence is selected, and its corresponding scale is used to determine the segmentation boundaries in time.

THESIS
DEVELOPING A TOOL FOR DANCE MOTION SYNTHESIS (2012)


Motion capture presents an interesting opportunity for the analysis and synthesis of movements in dance. We described a tool that uses concatenative synthesis of dance movement based on a library of prerecorded basic movements. Dance movements were first broken into discrete, small movements following the guidelines of Laban dance notation. Then these movements were performed by dancers and recorded using an 8-camera motion capture system. Finally, these (edited) sequences were placed in a 3D virtual environment where the user can synthesize movements to form a choreographed composition. Such a pedagogical tool provides a creative way to understand and study dance movements.

PROJECTS

IMPLEMENTATION OF A RAY TRACER IN JAVA (2013)


For a Graphics project, I implemented a ray tracer with support for quadric surfaces (spheres, ellipsoids, cylinders, boxes, 5 directional light sources, lighting models with ambient, diffuse and specular terms, as well as implementing effects caused by shadows, reflections and refractions. In addition, spatial data structures were used to make ray tracing more efficient and antialiasing was implemented to give cleaner output images.

PAPER PIANO (2012)


For my final project for Image and Video computing, we (2-member group) implemented an 8 key paper piano. An 8-key piano was printed on a piece of paper. The webcam was capable of detecting the keys, segmenting the hands of the user and determining which key was pressed and when. The program played back sounds based on the notes played on the paper piano.

WINCHESTER KITCHEN (2011)


Winchester Kitchen was a web application written using the LAMP (Linux, Apache, MySQL, PHP) stack that allowed users to select food available in a student run kitchen and place an order for pickup or delivery. This app was created for a group of students with a passion for cooking living in an on-campus apartment, who wished to cater to fellow students' desire to induldge in affordable out-of-college-dining cuisine. Users could select from an available selection of minors (appetizers) and majors (entrees). Administrators in the kitchen could see the orders as they were placed as well as edit the list as the orders were picked up or sent for delivery.
